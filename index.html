For Francine · a field guide in plain English

Bridge: a small start. You said the little quotes are told to you mid-hand, there's no time to write them down, and they're quickly forgotten. So here they all are, pinned down, each with its why underneath. A rule with a reason sticks.

Chapter one · Start from what you know

You already read cards in runs and sets

You know Machiavelli inside out, which means you already see a hand the way bridge players do: runs (K-Q-J of a suit) and sets (length, groups, shape). That skill is half of bridge. When this page says "a sequence" or "your longest suit", you already have the eye for it.

The genuinely new muscle is the trick: four cards, one from each player, highest of the led suit wins. Machiavelli builds melds on a table; bridge fights one small four-card battle thirteen times. Everything below is about winning more of those little battles than the other side.

One honest note before we start: bidding systems vary between clubs and teachers. Where this page gives numbers, they're the common UK (Acol) ones - your teacher's version always wins. What won't vary is the why, and the why is the bit that sticks.

Chapter four · Bidding, decoded

Bidding is just telling partner your shape

Forget mystique: the auction is a conversation in a language with about 30 words. Every bid says two things - roughly how strong I am and roughly what shape I am. Points measure the strength. Here's the count, on a real hand: tap each honour.

Ace = 4 · King = 3 · Queen = 2 · Jack = 1 (nothing else scores)
♠ 742
♥ 83
♦ 5
♣ 96
0 points so far Tap the aces, kings, queens and jacks.

Now the ladder you're trying to remember. It's not twenty rules - it's four numbers:

12+Open the bidding. "Partner, I have a proper hand."
6+Respond. "I heard you - I'm not empty-handed either."
25Between you: bid a game. 3NT or four of a major. This is the number the whole auction is trying to discover.
33Between you: slam territory. A rare treat. Don't live here.

And your exact question - "what tells me partner has a five-card suit?" - has a lovely one-line answer:

A suit bid once promises four. Bid it again: five. Again: six.

Partner doesn't repeat a suit for fun - repeating it is the only way to say "there are more of these than I first let on." So 1♠ then 2♠ = five spades. 1♠, 2♠, 3♠ = six. And a 1NT opening (12-14, balanced) says the opposite: "no long suit to shout about - I'm flat as a pancake."

Chapter six · Access to dummy

Entries: an ace isn't just a winner, it's a door

The game is called bridge. Treat dummy as an island: every high card that can win a trick over there is a bridge across - and once you've crossed and come back, that bridge is burned.

Look back at the 3NT hand above. Dummy's diamond helpers are worthless, but suppose it were dummy holding those lovely established diamonds instead. The question changes from "can I win tricks?" to "can I get there to cash them?"

That's why you'll hear "keep the ♥A in dummy till last". It isn't superstition. The ace has two jobs - winning a trick and carrying you across the table - and it can only do both if you spend it late. Cash it at trick two "because it's a winner" and dummy's remaining winners sit marooned on the island, waving.

The habit: before you play any ace or king, ask one question - "is this card also a door I still need?" That single question is 90% of entry management.

Chapter seven · The 😱 chapter

"Who has what?" - you don't memorise it, you count it

Here's the secret nobody says out loud: good players don't remember 52 cards. Not one of them. They count one suit at a time, to thirteen - usually just trumps.

Every suit has exactly 13 cards. You can see your hand and dummy's, so before a single card is played you already know how many the hidden hands hold: 13, minus mine, minus dummy's. If you have five trumps and dummy has three, the opponents have five. That's it - one small number, not a memory palace.

Then, as trumps get played, count the opponents' down: "five… three… one… none - safe." One running number. You already do exactly this in Machiavelli when you track what's out on the table; bridge just shrinks it to a single suit.

Start with trumps only. Counting one suit per hand, every hand, is how everyone at your club learned. The second suit comes free later, without trying. Nobody starts by counting four.

Chapter eight · Scoring, made small

Why everyone gallops towards 3NT

Scoring looks like an accountant's fever dream, but the whole engine is one number: 100 points wins a game. Each trick you contract for (beyond the first six) is worth:

♣ ♦ minors · 20 a trick ♥ ♠ majors · 30 a trick NT · 40 first, then 30

So the routes to 100 are:

3NT40 + 30 + 30 = 100 → only 9 tricks
4♥ 4♠30 × 4 = 120 → 10 tricks
5♣ 5♦20 × 5 = 100 → 11 tricks (!)
That's the entire mystery. 3NT is the cheapest game on the menu - nine tricks instead of eleven - which is why the whole room stampedes towards it. Game bonuses and doubles are just decoration on top of this one idea.
Chapter nine · About those silly mistakes

Everyone makes them. Truly everyone.

The players who look confident at your club are not making fewer decisions than you - they've just automated the quotes on this page so their brains are free to count one suit. That's the whole difference, and it comes from repetition, which is exactly what you said you weren't getting. Now the quotes live here instead of evaporating.

And bridge is a kind game to learn in one respect: a mistake costs one hand, and a completely fresh deal arrives a few minutes later. If you do just two things next time you play - make a plan at trick one and count trumps to thirteen - you'll already be playing better bridge than most of the table.
